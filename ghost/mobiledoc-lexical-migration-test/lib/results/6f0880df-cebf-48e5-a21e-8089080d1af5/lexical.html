Things That You Love To See:

• International Energy Agency Recommends Moratorium On New Fossil Fuel Projects: “Hard to overstate how big a deal it is for the IEA of all sources to say new oil and gas development needs to stop *this year* to reach net-zero by 2050 — and how far off it is from even some of the most progressive policy options on the table in the US,” tweeted climate reporter Kate Aronoff about the news.

• New York Bill Takes on Monopoly Power: “A groundbreaking new bill moving through the New York State Senate this week will, if enacted, greatly enhance the ability of the state and private plaintiffs to take on monopoly power and level the playing field for independent businesses in New York,” wrote the Institute For Local Self Reliance, an antitrust watchdog, about the bill.

• Biden Signs Sweeping Executive Order on Climate Finance: President Joe Biden signed a sweeping executive order this week calling on financial regulators to develop strategies for mitigating and responding to the risks posed by climate change to the financial sector. The order was praised by financial industry watchdogs like David Arkush, managing director of Public Citizen’s Climate Program, who said, “This order sends an important message that the Biden Administration understands the urgent need to address climate-related financial risks. It makes clear that financial regulators have a responsibility to mitigate those risks — including the ones that financial institutions create by financing emissions.”

Organizing Works:

• Philadelphia District Attorney Larry Krasner Trounces Police-backed Primary Challenger: Progressive District Attorney Larry Krasner won nearly two-thirds of the votes in his contest this week against his primary challenger, a police-union backed prosecutor who had called the Black Lives Matter movement “a pack of rabid animals.” The Intercept reports that “Krasner’s office pledged never to seek the death penalty, stopped requesting cash bail for low-level offenses, expanded diversion programs for some gun offenses, and stopped prosecuting marijuana use and sex work.”

• This Could Be the Start of a Rural Anti-Fracking Coalition: “Heartland residents like George who live close to oil and gas wells are less likely to support anti-fracking policies than people who live in places where fracking is not occurring (e.g., cities). However, there’s evidence that petroleum companies are losing public support — what’s sometimes referred to as their social license to operate,” notes The New Republic in its report on rural pushback to fracking in Pennsylvania.

• Amazon Extends Moratorium on Police Use of Facial Recognition Tech: Organizers who have been pressuring Amazon for over a year to extend its moratorium on selling facial recognition technology to the police announced a victory this week, after Amazon announced that it was indefinitely extending one-year moratorium that had been put in place after the police murder of George Floyd.

• Graduate Student Researchers In California Emerge From Pandemic Organized: About 17,000 graduate student researchers across ten campuses in the University of California system have announced a public unionization campaign. If the campaign is successful, the number of union members in the United States will increase by .1 percent.
