TODAY’S SCOOP:

As The Daily Posterpreviously reported, billionaire Charles Koch’s corporate empire has been plowing money into residential real estate since the COVID-19 pandemic started, while Koch-funded nonprofits have been pushing to end the federal eviction moratorium.

Democrats — after taking a cool million from a real estate mogul — allowed the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention’s (CDC) eviction ban to expire last weekend, before the Biden administration reversed course and announced a new moratorium on Tuesday to protect renters in areas of the U.S. that are seeing a surge in COVID-19 infections.

Two conservative groups backed by Koch that fought the original CDC policy are speaking out about the new moratorium now.

“Reissuing the CDC eviction moratorium is unlawful, irrational, & will devastate mom-and-pop housing providers everywhere,” the New Civil Liberties Alliance (NCLA) told the Washington Post. “NCLA will not stand for this. CDC never had legal authority to issue a nationwide eviction moratorium. Nothing has changed."

The Pacific Legal Foundation (PLF) tweeted, “The eviction moratoria slapped down across the nation will only deepen the crisis that will live on long after the pandemic has passed.” The organization added: “With every effort at social engineering, we create new problems. And the cycle goes on as when one gives a mouse a cookie.”

The Charles Koch Foundation gave$2 million to NCLA between 2017 and 2019, and $1.1 million to PLF.

TODAY’S FLASHBACK:

Two years into the American Revolutionary War, the Continental Army was on the verge of defeat — thanks to smallpox. Ninety percent of soldier deaths were due to the disease and other ailments. The surging virus was ravaging encampments, hindering troop movements, and hurting recruitment efforts. As General George Washington noted about smallpox in a letter, “we should have more to dread from [smallpox] than from the Sword of the Enemy.”

At the time, inoculating people with a less powerful form of smallpox was common in Europe and the British Army. But the Continental Congress, fearing the practice, banned the procedure among its troops. In February 1777, Washington decided to ignore the prohibition, writing to the governor of Virginia that the ban was “reprobated at this time, not only by the Consent and usage of [inoculations by] the greater part of the civilized World, but by our Interest and own experience of its utility.”

Washington then began history’s first mass inoculation campaign of an army in the middle of a war. The effort revitalized the Continental Army, spurred new recruitments, and is considered by some experts to be one of the main reasons the fledgling nation ended up winning its war for independence.
