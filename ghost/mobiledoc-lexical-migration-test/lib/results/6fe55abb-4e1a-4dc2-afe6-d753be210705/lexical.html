TODAY’S STAT:

In 2020, as the pandemic raged, over half of the nation’s 100 largest low-wage employers gave their executives massive raises while cutting wages for frontline workers, according to a new report from the Institute for Policy Studies.

“Among these 51 rule-rigging companies, average CEO compensation was $15.3 million in 2020, while median worker pay was $28,187 on average. The average CEO- worker pay ratio: 830 to 1,” the report found.

In March, lawmakers in the House and Senate introduced the Tax Excessive CEO Pay Act, which would increase taxes on companies that pay executives at least 50 times more than the median worker.

TODAY’S CLICKS:

  • TREASURY GUIDANCE ON STATE AND LOCAL AID: The Treasury Department has released a long-awaited interim final rule on how states and localities can use the $350 billion they will receive under the American Rescue Plan. In addition to delineating how the funds can be used, the rule further clarifies how a provision of the ARP that prevents the funds from being used to subsidize tax cuts will be implemented. The guidance sets a floor for tax revenue at 2019 revenue levels indexed to inflation, so that if states or localities slash any type of tax revenue — including corporate tax subsidies — below 2019 levels, they must either cut spending by the same amount or return the ARP aid they received equivalent to that amount. The guidance allows for some wiggle room — one percent of that 2019 amount — so that states can enact some small tax cuts and still be within the rules.
  • CONGRESS THREATENS TO DERAIL BIDEN’S TAX PLAN: Biden has proposed tax hikes on the wealthy and corporations to fund his infrastructure bill, but defectors in Congress from his own party are threatening to withhold their votes for such tax hikes in response to donor pressure. Meanwhile, Democratic congressional leadership is pushing for a repeal of the SALT cap, which could cost about $600 billion, the benefits of which would largely go to the wealthiest Americans — not to mention many of the lawmakers pushing to repeal the cap.
  • SENATORS BEAT THE MARKET: U.S. Senators are engaged in frequent stock trading and tend to outperform the stock market, according to a new report by Unusual Whales, which analyzes stock trading. Sen. Elizabeth Warren has proposed a ban on all stock trading by federal lawmakers, who have access to information not available to the general public and yet are hard to hold accountable to insider trading laws.
