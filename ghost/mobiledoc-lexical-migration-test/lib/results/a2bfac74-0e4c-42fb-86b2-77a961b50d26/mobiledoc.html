As the health care crisis worsens, it is worth looking back on the long struggle to get America to join the rest of the industrialized world in guaranteeing health care for all.

In the lead up to his U.S. Senate run, Barack Obama cast himself as a proponent of single-payer health care, saying Democrats just had to first win the presidency, the House and the Senate and then it would be possible.

Within two years of being elected to the Senate, he told me in an interview that he was more skeptical of single-payer. Then, when Democrats actually did win the presidency, the House and the Senate, Obama’s new presidential administration bragged about going out of its way to make sure the Affordable Care Act would prevent the creation of a single-payer health care system. Obama’s top aides also pressed Senate Democrats to accede to Joe Lieberman’s efforts to kill a public option.

But that didn’t stop at least a few Democrats from mustering the courage to keep pushing.

Rewind 11 years and read this summer 2009 story from the Missoulian, in which a Democratic governor from a deep red state used his introductory speech of Obama to publicly pressure the new president, the Democratic Party and its corporate-friendly establishment to support a Medicare for All-style health care program:

As Gov. Brian Schweitzer warmed up the crowd Friday for President Barack Obama, he paid a lengthy compliment to a health-care system that leading Democrats, including the president, have declared "off the table" as a reform here: the Canadian single-payer system.

"Did you know that, just 300 miles north of here, did you know they offered universal health care 62 years ago?" he said, referring to Canada's system of providing government-funded health insurance for all citizens…

Most of the crowd of 1,300 in the Gallatin Field Airport hangar roared in approval.

Schweitzer also noted how former Saskatchewan Premier Tommy Douglas, considered the father of the Canadian health system, was recently voted in a nationwide poll as Canada’s most popular person in history…

Minutes later, Obama made it clear that he does not favor adopting such a system in the United States.

He said: “The majority of people get their health insurance on the job, so let's build on that system that already exists — because for us to completely change that, it would be too disruptive.”

Since Obama left office, millions of Americans have lost their health care coverage during a historic pandemic, as health care industry profits have skyrocketed. For his part, Obama has come back around, calling Medicare For All a “good, new idea.” It’s too bad he didn’t have that revelation when he was actually in office.

The effort by Schweitzer (whose campaigns I worked on) offer two important lessons.

First and foremost, economic populism and electability are not at odds in the way Democratic consultants often suggest they are. Schweitzer pushed Canadian-style single-payer health care and also won statewide elections in Montana. Arguably, he won those elections during the Bush years precisely because he didn’t sound like a standard corporate-appeasing Democrat.

Second, Democrats do not just have to fall in line behind a president focused on defending health insurance and pharmaceutical companies. There are ways for elected officials to push their party’s president, if they so choose.

For most of the last 40 years, they’ve chosen to do the opposite — Democrats have loyally fallen in line behind their party’s presidents on everything from financial deregulation to bank bailouts. But it doesn’t have to be that way — and if there is any hope for real progress on health care, it can’t be that way.

Considering the political power of the health care industry, securing even a modest public option — much less Medicare for All — is going to require local, state and national Democratic elected officials to find the courage to pressure their own party’s president, whether that president likes it or not.
