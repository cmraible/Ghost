TODAY’S SCOOP:

Weeks after Democratic Sens. Kyrsten Sinema and Joe Manchin voted to kill a $15 minimum wage, they were rewarded with donations from the corporate lobby group leading the opposition to the measure, according to newly disclosed campaign finance records reviewed by The Daily Poster.

Sinema and Manchin were two of the eight Democrats who voted with Republicans last month to block the Senate from even considering Sen. Bernie Sanders’ measure to fulfill Democrats’ campaign promise to raise the wage to $15. In the last week of March, $2,500 checks were delivered to both of them by the political action committee for the U.S. Chamber of Commerce, which has been running the opposition campaign to the higher wage. Sinema also pulled in a $1,000 donation from the international law firm Steptoe & Johnson, which has recently been lobbying on wage issues for food giant Starkist.

Sinema and Manchin have both raised objections to the idea of eliminating the Senate filibuster, and their stand serves other corporate interests that also recently poured cash into their campaign coffers. For example: The health care industry has been working to halt a public health insurance option, a measure that won’t get any support from Republicans.

In the last quarter alone, Sinema received $33,000 from the PACs of major health care, pharmaceutical, and insurance corporations and trade associations — including insurance group Blue Cross Blue Shield, hospital chains HCA Healthcare and LifePoint Health, and the hospital lobbying group Federation of American Hospitals. Manchin received PAC donations from Blue Cross as well as the American Hospital Association and Alston & Bird, the latter of which lobbies for major health care industry clients.

Similarly, leaving the filibuster in place will likely put a halt to any major climate and environmental legislation. Sinema received $11,000 from PACs of large energy companies including electric utilities Duke Energy and Southern Company, while Manchin received donations from the PACs of Northwest Natural Gas ($2,500) and Greenberg Traurig ($4,000), which has lobbied for energy giants such as Peabody, Centerpoint and Covanta.

TODAY’S STAT:

Climate change is increasingly factoring into Americans’ living decisions. According to a new survey by real estate brokerage Redfin, nearly half of Americans who plan on relocating considered the increased frequency and intensity of natural disasters and/or extreme temperatures in their decision-making process, with 36 percent citing rising sea levels.

TODAY’S CLICKS:

• CONGRESSIONAL PROGRESSIVES URGE BIG THINKING ON INFRASTRUCTURE: There is an ongoing battle in D.C over President Joe Biden’s new infrastructure plans. Congressional progressives are calling for a single ambitious spending package that addresses physical infrastructure as well as “care” infrastructure — things like child care and paid family leave — and invests $10 trillion in green energy over the next decade. Conservative Democrats, meanwhile, have threatened to hold up any package unless it includes a tax cut for wealthy homeowners.

• BIDEN FACES PRESSURE OVER YEMEN: Biden is facing pressure from a bipartisan group of lawmakers to bring about an end to Saudi Arabia’s blockade of Yemen, which has led to extreme famine and the world’s worst humanitarian crisis. "Ending this practice will boost Yemen's economy, de-escalate the conflict, and prevent this humanitarian catastrophe from worsening — all important U.S. objectives,” lawmakers wrote in a letter to Secretary of State Antony Blinken. Earlier this month, 70 progressive lawmakers sent a similar letter to Biden, urging him to pressure Saudi Arabia into ending the blockade. The state department, meanwhile, denies there is a blockade in place.

• WORLD LEADERS, NOBEL LAUREATES PUSH TO SUSPEND VAX PATENTS: A group of 175 former world leaders and Nobel laureates are urging Biden to waive the patents for COVID-19 vaccines in order to boost vaccination efforts globally as the pandemic continues to surges. Nearly 60 countries are backing a measure proposed at the World Trade Organization to allow nations to temporarily override patents for COVID-related medical products. The list of signatories includes former UK prime minister Gordon Brown, former French president François Hollande, and former president of the Soviet Union Mikhail Gorbachev.

• NANCY PELOSI BALKS AT COURT PACKING: Hours after congressional Democrats introduced legislation to expand the number of Supreme Court justices from 9 to 13, House Speaker Nancy Pelosi poured cold water on the idea. Pelosi said she does not support the bill and would not bring it to the floor.
