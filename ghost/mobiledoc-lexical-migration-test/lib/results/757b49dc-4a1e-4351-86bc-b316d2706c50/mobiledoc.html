Follow The Story:

• The Dangers of Ducking the Health Care Fight (American Prospect) — “Shying away from a fight with the health industry today will just lead to a more impossible fight tomorrow. And yet, that pretty well articulates what the Biden administration’s political theory is right now. For the past 50 years, incoming Democratic administrations and the party base have made health care a top policy priority; the Biden administration has not. The American Rescue Plan did nothing on health care other than increasing federal subsidies for the purchase of health insurance, and only for a limited time period tied to the pandemic. The White House had to be dragged kicking and screaming just to extend those subsidies in its current American Families Plan proposal. Other Biden campaign promises, like a public option or a plan to deal with prescription drug costs, have thus far been left on the cutting-room floor.”

• A Way Of Thinking About Israel That Isn’t Helpful And May Even Be Dangerous (LA Times) — “I am an American, born and raised here, and as such I am obligated to cheer what I admire, criticize what I don’t, serve if needed and participate in American civil society knowing that what’s best for us as a people may not always be best for me as an individual. That’s fine. My responsibility is not to “love” the U.S.; it’s to be a citizen. Same thing with Israel.”

• Young American Jews Have Reached a Tipping Point With Israel (Rolling Stone) — “Four days after Israeli police invaded Al-Aqsa Mosque, a Muslim holy site in East Jerusalem, the questions and the words finally came to me and I took to Twitter to share my newly crystallized feelings to see if they would resonate. “Being an American Jew is a mindfuck,” the now-viral thread began; it goes on to describe how American Jews are raised to believe in the infallibility of Israel, how the intergenerational trauma of the Holocaust shapes our view of the region, and how our history of oppression should make us even more empathetic to the displacement and killing of Palestinians.”

• The Particular Psychology of Destroying a Planet (New Yorker) — “How is it that some people, or corporations, can knowingly perpetuate the damage? Or, as people routinely ask me, ‘Don’t they have grandchildren?’”

• The Case for Putting Climate Labels on Meat (Modern Farmer) — “The question, then, is how to encourage US consumers to do just that in a way that is both environmentally productive and politically feasible. I offer one possible solution: The federal government could make climate labelling mandatory for food, not unlike what it did with nutritional content in the early 1990s. An industry-wide system would be ideal, but to speed implementation while maintaining impact, the Biden administration should first target those foods most responsible for heating our planet: meat and dairy.”

• How New York City Is Saving Its Local News Outlets (New York Times) — “At a time when newsrooms nationwide are laying off reporters and some are closing down, a program begun by Mayor Bill de Blasio’s administration has been helping to sustain small, independent media outlets in every corner of the city. In May 2019 he signed an executive order requiring city agencies to direct at least half their budgets for digital and print advertising to community newspapers and websites. These media outlets are often their communities’ most trusted sources of information. They publish in more than 30 languages throughout the five boroughs, serving immigrants, ethnic and religious groups and communities of color. It has been a resounding success.”

• The White Men Who Wanted to Be Victims (New Republic) — “The title of Joseph Darda’s new book, How White Men Won the Culture Wars, may land awkwardly for weary followers of recursive debates over cancel culture, wokeness, and the like. The loudest and most visible partisans in these battles are aggrieved white men, insisting that they’re scapegoats in unhinged identity-driven witch hunts and eagerly putting themselves forward as martyrs in ugly confrontations over free speech. How is it, then, that this demographic emerged as the victors of the modern American culture wars and managed to leverage that success into ongoing, ever-renewable plaints of grievance?”

• Can Democracy Survive Amazon? (Capital & Main) — “We are watching the lifeblood and commerce being sucked out of all these different communities around the country and concentrating it in a small handful of cities that are so wealthy that they are borderline uninhabitable for most people. We have to ask what it means for our democracy to have such extraordinary concentration of corporate power and wealth.”

A Message From Sirota:

Friends:

This week brought more bad news for journalism: A vulture hedge fund is taking control of the Tribune Company, and if past actions are any indication, that could mean more mass layoffs of rank-and-file reporters, on top of the 16,000+ journalism jobs that were lost in 2020. That’s bad news for democracy.

Of late, there has been a lot of hype about the possibilities of new independent platforms to liberate writers and rescue journalism from this downward spiral. I’m cautiously optimistic about that prospect — but with some reservations.

The rise of new platforms can be a significant game-changing development in the effort to fix media — but only if the new platforms are used to support journalism rather than just hot takes, and only if readers are willing to support teams of journalists rather than just individual opinionists.

Put another way: We have to do the hard work of building new journalism institutions, and resist the easy path of just trying to create new pundit celebrities.

This is what we’re trying to do at The Daily Poster, and this past week illustrated the potential. From our Koch investigation, to our reporting on climate policy, to our scrutiny of health care politics, our small team of reporters broke big stories, which were then amplified by other media outlets and political leaders, including Sen. Bernie Sanders.

None of this would be possible without you — our supporting subscribers. You are why I am increasingly optimistic that we can continue growing our support, building our newsroom, and contributing to a larger movement for a better media.

There are no shortcuts here. Building an engaged readership that supports real journalism that challenges power is a long, slow process. And the “engaged” part is critical — we want and need your input, feedback and tips. You should always use the comments at the bottom of articles to provide us feedback — we read every single one. And you should always feel free to email us at editor@dailyposter.com — we take every email to heart.

Thank you for being part of this movement for a better media, which is part of the larger movement for a better world. This is not a short sprint, it is a marathon — but it is worth the effort. Onward.

Rock the boat,

Sirota
