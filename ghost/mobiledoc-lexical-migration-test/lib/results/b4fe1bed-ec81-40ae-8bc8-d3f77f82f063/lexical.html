Things That You Love To See:

• Scabby Is Free (National Labor Relations Board) —“Today, a majority of the Board found that a union’s use of rats or other inflatables, along with other bannering activity, does not violate the National Labor Relations Act, even when targeting a neutral employer.” Vice has the story of the battle over the iconic inflatable rat.

• ‘Right To Repair’ Unanimously Backed By Federal Trade Commission (Huffington Post) — “Americans would be freer to repair their broken cellphones, computers, videogame consoles and even tractors themselves, or to use independent repair shops, under changes being eyed by federal regulators. The regulators maintain that restrictions have steered consumers into manufacturers’ and sellers’ repair networks or led them to replace products before the end of their useful lives.”

• PG&E Will Spend At Least $15 Billion Burying Power Lines (Associated Press) — “Pacific Gas & Electric plans to bury 10,000 miles (16,000 kilometers) of its power lines in an effort to prevent its fraying grid from sparking wildfires when electrical equipment collides with millions of trees and other vegetation across drought-stricken California.”

• The New Brandeis Movement Has Its Moment (The American Prospect) — With the appointment of Jonathan Kanter as assistant attorney general for antitrust at the Department of Justice, as well as Lina Khan as chair of the Federal Trade Commission, “the Biden administration now has antitrust advocates with a track record of actually pursuing antitrust in powerful positions, rather than former toadies ready to engage in the corporate capture that has been the norm under recent presidents, both Republican and Democrat,” The American Prospect reports.

Organizing Works:

• Biden Issues Executive Order To Lower Drug Prices(Washington Post) —President Joe Biden has issued an executive order that would lower drug prices by allowing states to import drugs from Canada, something that progressives have been advocating for for decades, as The Daily Posterpreviously reported. But, Natalie Shure warns in The New Republic, importing drugs isn’t a panacea — only universal health care coverage can ensure that everyone has access to the medicine they need.

• New D.C. Councilmembers Help Pass Tax Increase on Wealthy(Washington City Paper) —“The D.C. Council voted 8-5 to support a tax increase for individuals who earn $250,000 or more a year. The estimated $100 million in revenue will fund early childhood education from birth to age three, 2,400 housing vouchers, and a monthly basic income for certain families.”

• Senator Mark Kelly Says He Supports ‘Overall Goals’ Of PRO Act (Huffington Post) — “A key Senate Democrat hinted Wednesday that he would back his party’s effort to overhaul labor law and boost union membership through landmark reforms.” Read more about the organizers pressuring Sen. Mark Kelly, D-Ariz., in The Daily Poster.

• Albany Passes ‘Good Cause Eviction,’ In a First for New York(New York Focus) —“On Monday, Albany became the first municipality in New York to pass ‘good cause’ eviction protections, which would bar landlords from evicting tenants or increasing rents more than 5 percent without justification… Albany was the first stop in a campaign that housing rights organizers are mounting in cities across upstate New York, as New York Focus reported last week.”
