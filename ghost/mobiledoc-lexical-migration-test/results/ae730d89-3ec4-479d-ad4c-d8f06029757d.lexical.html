So Long, Michelle Childs

Judge Michelle Childs, of the U.S. District Court for the District of South Carolina, has been passed over for the Supreme Court. Biden had pledged to name the first Black woman to the bench after Justice Stephen Breyer announced his upcoming retirement. Childs was a top contender, boasting the support of both Sen. Lindsey Graham (R-S.C.) and Rep. Jim Clyburn (D-S.C.). But progressive activists and labor unions urged the administration to find another judge given Childs’ record as a management-side labor attorney with a history of punitive criminal justice rulings.

Graham, who had predicted that Childs would get more than 10 Republican votes, was none too pleased with the news that Biden was going in a different direction. On Friday, the senator took to Twitter to bemoan the defeat of his preferred judge, writing that “the attacks by the Left on Judge Childs from South Carolina apparently worked” and noting, “the Harvard-Yale train to the Supreme Court continues to run unabated.”

On Friday, Biden instead announced he was nominating Judge Kentaji Brown Jackson of the Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit to replace Breyer.

Brown, a former public defender, could prove to be a better choice. Earlier this month, in her first appeals court ruling, Brown sided with public sector unions against the Federal Labor Relations Authority when the agency tried to heighten bargaining requirements for public-sector workers. And while Jackson has also come under fire for refusing to certify a 2017 class action lawsuit filed by thousands of Black employees against Lockheed Martin for discrimination, her overall work history pleases many progressives. Congressional candidate and former Bernie Sanders surrogate Nina Turner, for example, has endorsed Jackson’s nomination.

Organizing Works

Workers at the Jon Donaire Desserts plant in Santa Fe Springs, California, have prevailed after a 113-day strike, securing a $3.10 wage increase over four years and blocking a plan to cut their health care benefits. More than 150 workers, who make ice cream cakes for companies like Baskin Robbins, Cold Stone Creamery, Walmart, Red Robin, and Safeway, had gone on strike in early November, demanding a $1 raise every year for three years and no healthcare cost increases.

Plant owner Rich Products Corporation, which reported more than $4 billion in revenue in the first year of the pandemic, relented this week. In response, Miguel Perez, who has worked at the plant for more than a decade, told More Perfect Union that the workers would be going back to work “with our heads held high” and that they “feel great.”

Americans Don’t Like Banning Books

Right-wing groups across the country have been trying to use critical race theory to rile up parents and take over school boards and state governments. A recent report from the American Library Association found that there were 330 attempts to ban books across the country last fall, representing a significant increase from recent years.

But a recent poll suggests that the right is losing this culture war. A new CBS-YouGov survey found that 85 percent of Americans do not support banning books from schools that contain political ideas they disagree with, 87 percent do not support banning books that discuss race or slavery, and 83 percent do not support banning books that criticize people or events in U.S. history.

Dakota Access Legal Battle Ends In Pipeline Loss

After six long years, the legal battle over the Dakota Access Pipeline is over. The Supreme Court declined to take up an appeal by Energy Transfer, the operator of the pipeline, after a lower court handed a victory to the Standing Rock Sioux and other North Dakota tribes.

The ruling, which will now stand, invalidated a key federal permit for the pipeline on the grounds that it violated the National Environmental Policy Act. The court required the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers to conduct a comprehensive environmental review of the pipeline’s route underneath Lake Oahe, on the border of North and South Dakota, which Indigenous communities consider sacred and have historically relied on. Although the court battle was won, however, the war of the pipeline’s fate continues. The pipeline has been operating since 2017 and will continue to do so while the environmental review is completed.

NLRB Tells Starbucks to Kick Rocks

Starbucks baristas are unionizing. Workers at several stories in upstate New York have petitioned for union elections, following the lead of their colleagues in the Buffalo area. But the company has been trying to slow the elections, asking the National Labor Relations Board not to move forward with voting on the grounds that all stores in the region should vote together in one election. Unfortunately for the company, its attorneys missed a crucial deadline with the NLRB after they failed to turn in all of their paperwork on time. The company missed the cutoff just eight minutes.

As a result of the missed deadline, the NLRB’s regional director ordered that the company was precluded “from litigating any of the issues raised in its untimely Submission,” despite the company’s attorneys arguing the union, Workers United, “suffered no prejudice” from the delay. The company can appeal the regional director’s order in Washington.

Meanwhile, labor committee chairs from 17 states and D.C. sent a letter to Starbucks’ CEO on Wednesday in support of the Starbucks Workers United union, urging the company to “follow the letter and spirit of the law by allowing your employees free choice in joining a union.”

Accountability By Kaepernick

Know Your Rights Camp, a group founded by former NFL quarterback Colin Kaepernick, is providing free second autopsies for families of people killed in circumstances involving police. The group will provide second opinions that have been previously widely unavailable due to high costs. Families often have to rely on the work of the local medical examiner or coroner, opening the door for individual biases to impact the outcome.

Nature’s Backup

Scientists recently made groundbreaking discoveries while studying sea beds that could prove useful in the fight against climate change. Researchers found that sea beds are host to far greater biodiversity than the oceans above them with many unknown lifeforms. Among the organisms found were plankton that fall to the seafloor and sequester carbon for millions of years. According to the paper published by the researchers, the findings “highlight previously underappreciated taxa that may be keystone drivers of the biological carbon pump.”
