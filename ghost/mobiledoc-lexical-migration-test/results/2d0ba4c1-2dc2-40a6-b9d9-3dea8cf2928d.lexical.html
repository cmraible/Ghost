TODAY’S SCOOP:

In recent months, one-time Republican Minnesota Congressman Erik Paulsen has had a lot to say about Democrats' proposal to allow Medicare to use its bulk purchasing power to negotiate lower drug prices — none of it positive. As he wrote for RealClearPolicy in December, “let’s hope for the sake of patients that legislators drop these worrisome provisions, and instead focus on policies that will make drugs more affordable.”

And in October, he penned a column for RealClearHealth warning that the legislation would stifle pharmaceutical innovation. “This is no time to handcuff an industry in the business of inventing life-saving medical technologies — including the vaccines that are saving the world from the COVID-19 pandemic,” Paulsen wrote.

Interesting he should mention vaccines that are “saving the world.” Just last summer, Paulsen scoffed at President Joe Biden’s support for a World Trade Organization waiver to force American COVID-19 vaccine manufacturers to share intellectual property rights for the life-saving vaccines with the global community, arguing such a move would “accelerate the timeline for ‘Made in China 2025.’”

But what Paulsen has conveniently failed to mention in any of his columns is the fact that he also serves as the chairman of the Institute for Gene Therapies, a dark money group whose corporate advisory council members include COVID-19 vaccine makers Pfizer and Johnson & Johnson, as well as other pharmaceutical heavy-hitters like Astellas Pharma and Vertex Pharmaceuticals.

And for all Big Pharma’s talk of opposing prescription drug pricing reform on the basis of wanting greater innovation and patient access, we know the real reason they are battling a government payer’s ability to negotiate lower prices is because they hope to continue profiting off a business scheme that price-gouges patients for life-saving medication.

TODAY’S CLICKS:

  • BRONX BUILDING RECEIVED CODE VIOLATION NOTICES BEFORE FIRE: New reporting from The Intercept shows the Bronx apartment building that caught fire and killed at least 17 people on Sunday ”was the subject of at least two maintenance code violations last year related to broken or defective fire retardant material in its walls.” The violation notices were issued last April and October by the New York City Department of Housing Preservation and Development. In the last decade, the building has received at least 169 violation notices. “It’s no coincidence that the owners of this building were also deeply politically connected despite hundreds of maintenance and safety complaints,” said New York Assembly Member Yuh Line Niou, who represents the 65th district covering lower Manhattan. One of the building owners is a member of new Mayor Eric Adams’ transition team.
  • A SMALL VICTORY IN THE FACEBOOK MONOPOLY FIGHT: Yesterday, a judge ruled against Facebook’s request to throw out a monopoly lawsuit the U.S. government filed in December 2020 targeting the tech giant. The Federal Trade Commission’s antitrust complaint, which was refiled in August after being dismissed by a judge last June, will move forward after U.S. District Judge James Boasberg denied Facebook’s motion to scrap the case. “The agency has also explained that Facebook not only possesses monopoly power, but that it has willfully maintained that power through anticompetitive conduct,” Boasberg wrote of the revised complaint. The case is the latest in an ongoing effort by the Biden Administration and FTC Chair Lina Khan to rein in Big Tech.

  • “IN 2021, CONGRESS BEAT THE MARKET!”: A new report from UnusualWhales.com reveals that in the past year alone, U.S. lawmakers traded stocks, options, and other financial assets more than ever, with hundreds of millions of dollars exchanged and huge gains delivered to legislators and their families. The trading report, which summarizes all financial disclosures from Dec. 1, 2020 to Dec. 22, 2021, shows that members timed their 2021 trading around notable legislative events like Senate passage of the bipartisan infrastructure bill — along the way making millions in sectors affected by the legislation. Some of the busiest traders included House Reps. Josh Gottheimer (D-N.J.), Marie Newman (D-Ill.), Susie Lee (D-Nev.) and Zoe Lofgren (D-Calif.), as well as Sens. John Boozman (R-Ark.) and Tommy Tuberville (R-Ala.). “Our politicians traded in huge volumes and amounts while being privy to closed-door meetings and negotiations on committees in the House and Senate,” the report noted. Sen. Jon Ossoff (D-Ga.) is expected to introduce legislation that would ban members of Congress from trading stocks while in office.
  • MAINE GOVERNOR DASHES FARMWORKERS’ HOPES OF UNIONIZING: Last week, Maine’s Democratic Gov. Janet Mills vetoed legislation that would have given agricultural workers in her state the right to unionize, pinning her decision on farmers’ supposed inability to understand the bill’s complexity. Gov. Mills’ veto makes her the second Democratic governor after California’s Gavin Newsom to nix legislation that would have carved a pathway for farmworkers to organize and collectively bargain for better wages and working conditions. As Jacobin noted, Mills’ “recent veto of basic rights for farmworkers is a reminder that plenty of opposition to workers' rights comes from the country’s most powerful liberal representatives.”
  • CHAMBER OF COMMERCE MEMBERS GUILTY OF MASSIVE WRONGDOING: Over 100 members of the U.S. Chamber of Commerce — the nation’s largest business lobby — violated the law nearly 16,000 times and paid a whopping $154 billion in penalties since 2000, according to a new analysis from consumer watchdog group Public Citizen. The report, which looks at data from the Corporate Research Project of Good Jobs First’s Violation Tracker, found that megabanks JPMorgan Chase, Citigroup, and Wells Fargo account for more than half of the total owed in penalties. Johnson & Johnson, Occidental Petroleum, and Facebook were also among the companies that paid sizable penalties for misdeeds from state and federal authorities.
