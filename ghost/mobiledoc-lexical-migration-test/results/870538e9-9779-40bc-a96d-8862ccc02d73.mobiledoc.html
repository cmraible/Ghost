SEO — death of AMP, Google News Showcase goes desktop

A few people have asked me to write about the Core Web Vitals update that Google's implementing — I will get to that soon. However, in the meantime, here's one core (ho, ho) fact that you need to know: AMP ceases to be a requirement for appearing in the news carousel.

The upside of this is that it might well now be better focusing developer time on making your mobile pages better, rather than building out AMP and the separate set of pages that requires.

Twitter — lots of news

Paid Twitter is slowly making its way around the world. I'm not sure how compelling the offer is right now, but they're bound to extend it over time.

This is really interesting, though: Twitter is taking away as much friction as possible in getting people to sign up for your Revue-based newsletter. This is a really practical, intelligent integration of an acquisition that will make Revue more compelling for people with large Twitter followings.

Outside rethinks the bundle

Those of you who have studied with me in any way will be familiar with the tendency of digital media to atomise — breaking publishing bundles down to their constituent parts. However, rebundling in new forms is the next logical step, and this is a really interesting attempt in the consumer sector.

Building a bundle of content around a particular interest, but letting subscribers choose the elements of that content that most appeal to them, might be a successful answer to the “flight to niche” problem that has eroded the viability of more generalist titles.

Or, of course, it might fall foul of the general problem of being too generalist in a crowded marketplace that causes the flight to niche in the first place. Worth watching.

Substack's comic ambitions

In our self-important myopia, journalists often think everything is about, well, us. But it's not, and even Substack, the latest obsession of the media development classes, has its eyes on wider markets. They've hired Nick Spencer, the current writer of Amazing Spider-Man and the man who turned Captain America into a fascist supporter of Hydra, to help steer a new wave of comics delivered via Substack newsletter. He's bowing out from his current Marvel gig to do this.

Substack comics could actually be a lot of fun, and probably a lot less controversial than the wave of culture warriors that recently joined the platform.
