The TMI Weekend Reading List — 7/5/20

Stuff I’m reading:

• Someone is Wrong on the Internet: A Study in Pandemic Distraction (LitHub) — A perfect essay on pandemic procrastination.

• Is BlackRock the New Vampire Squid? (The New Republic) — This Wall Street behemoth is now about 3 degrees of separation from everything in America — and it is using its power to make the climate crisis worse.

• The Man from Sullivan and Cromwell (Type Investigations) — We’ve been doing a bunch of reporting on private equity taking over everything in the country. One of the people at the center of that is SEC Chairman Jay Clayton — and this is the definitive piece on exactly why he is such a terrifying figure.

• Barbara Lee Would Make a Great Vice Presidential Nominee (The Nation) — Joe Biden says he will nominate a woman for vice president. Here’s the case for why this progressive hero should be on his short list.

• Who Died For Your Dinner? (Buzzfeed) — An important investigation that “reveals the extent to which the virus — and the nation’s inadequate response to it — has infected, sickened, and even killed workers up and down the nation's food supply chains as they work to keep our refrigerators full.”

• In the Covid-19 Economy, You Can Have a Kid or a Job. You Can’t Have Both (New York Times) — As Americans are being forced back to work in a lethal pandemic, few are asking what working parents are supposed to do with kids who are stuck at home.

• The ‘Comma Effect’ On Bias and Black Lives (the Undefeated) — A grammatical pause that helps explain how racism thrives.

• How the American Worker Got Fleeced (Businessweek) — The story of how American workers were uniquely vulnerable to being economically wiped out by the pandemic.

Stuff to watch/listen to:

• Bag Man (MSNBC) — I’ve been enjoying this deep dive into the forgotten corruption scandal that sank Spiro Agnew.

• Snowpiercer — This is Bong Joon Ho’s 2013 film that came before his blockbuster hit “Parasite.” I just got around to watching it because it is available on Netflix — and not surprisingly, it is fantastic. Assuming you can handle a dystopian sci fi during the pandemic, make sure to watch it.
