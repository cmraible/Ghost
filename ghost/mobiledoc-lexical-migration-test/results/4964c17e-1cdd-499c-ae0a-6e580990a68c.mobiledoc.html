TODAY’S BIG NEWS:

CEOs got a huge boost during the pandemic while worker compensation effectively stagnated. According to new data from the Economic Policy Institute, CEOs saw a 16 percent increase in compensation in 2020 while the COVID-19 swept the country. Workers, who were left to face the virus with little protection, saw their pay increase by a mere 1.8 percent — barely beating the 1.4 percent inflation rate.

In other words, while corporate executives were raking in money and corporate America was offering lip service to frontline workers, calling them heroes in emotional ads, those same workers weren’t benefitting at all from the wealth they were generating.

Adding insult to injury, amid reports that many workers are declining to return to low-paying jobs, corporate America has been lobbying to end the $300 pandemic unemployment assistance. Last month, the U.S. Chamber of Commerce, the biggest business lobbying group in the country, joined the fight to strip workers of their federal unemployment benefits and force them back into unsafe, low-wage jobs.

TODAY’S STAT:

Survival checks substantially improved American life according to a new analysis of U.S. Census data. After the $600 and $1,400 checks went out, there was a 42 percent decline in the number of households with children experiencing food shortages from January to April, a 43 percent decline in financial instability, and a more than 20 percent decline in anxiety and depression. However, these improvements were only temporary, because the checks were one-time payments.

TODAY’S FLASHBACK:

Eleven years ago, on June 15, 2010, President Barack Obama addressed the nation to discuss the BP Deepwater Horizon oil spill, which he called “the worst environmental disaster America has ever faced.” Promising BP would pay for the damage, the president said that disaster proved the time had come to “embrace a clear energy future.”

"We can't afford not to change how we produce and use energy — because the long-term costs to our economy, our national security, and our environment are far greater," Obama said.

Five years and two months later, however, Obama allowed Shell to drill for oil in the Chukchi Sea off the coast of Alaska. As he was leaving office roughly a year and a half later, Obama tried to reverse himself, banning new drilling in the Arctic before Donald Trump took office.

On Tuesday, the U.S. Interior Department announced that Biden is suspending all oil and gas leases in Alaska’s National Wildlife Refuge in order to better study the environmental impacts. However, days earlier, the administration filed a brief to defend in court a Trump-era oil and gas drilling project by ConocoPhillips in the North Slope of Alaska, which environmental groups claim did not take into account environmental impacts. The Interior Department is arguing that the Trump administration complied with environmental requirements and that ConocoPhillips’ lease is valid. The project, known as Willow, is set to produce 100,000 barrels of oil per day for the next 30 years. In January, Biden issued an executive order to examine “legal deficiencies” in the leasing program.
