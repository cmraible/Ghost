The five largest military contractors in America received $2 trillion in public dollars from Congress over the course of the war in Afghanistan, according to an analysis from Stephen Semler of the Security Policy Reform Institute.

TODAY’S NEWS:

When Biden pledged in June that any program that received funding in the bipartisan Senate infrastructure bill would not be included in Democrats’ reconciliation bill, the party quietly acquiesced to the arrangement — meaning they signed off on massive cuts to vital environmental justice initiatives.

Biden’s “no double-dipping” commitment meant cutting almost all funding for an initiative to reconnect minority communities split apart by highway projects and cutting a program to replace lead pipes by 67 percent, among others.

Read climate strategist Brad Johnson’s full blog post on the matter.

TODAY’S CLICKS:

  • PURDUE PHARMA PRESSURES DOJ NOT TO APPEAL SETTLEMENT: Purdue Pharma, the manufacturer of OxyContin, has undertaken a stealth operation to stop the Department of Justice (DOJ) from appealing its bankruptcy settlement, which protects the Sackler family and their associates from legal liability for the opioid crisis. “We collectively speak for the overwhelming majority of the state and local governments, organizations, and individuals harmed by Purdue and the Sacklers,” noted the company in a letter urging the DOJ to accept the settlement, according to a copy obtained by NPR. The letter, which was sent to groups supportive of the bankruptcy arrangement, did not disclose Purdue’s role in organizing the appeal.
  • BENEFITS CLIFF LOOMS: Biden’s American Rescue Plan, the COVID-19 relief package passed in March, temporarily expanded the country’s social safety net in ways that were incredibly effective — and reflected the success of progressives in rejecting decades of austerity politics. But now, millionsof Americans are staring down a financial cliff as those benefits are set to end. The first cliff will arrive on Labor Day, when 7.5 million people will lose federal unemployment benefits, a loss that will affect 20 million Americans, including dependents of people receiving the aid. This the largest such benefit cutoff in recent history — and with the nation struggling through a resurgent pandemic, many say it’s far too early for the program to end.
  • FOSSIL FUEL INTERESTS TWEET THROUGH FOSSIL-FUEL-CREATED DISASTER: As Hurricane Ida, powered by the burning of fossil fuels, pummeled the United States, the fossil fuel industry took to Twitter. ExxonMobil, the company that invested in climate change research in the 1970s and then spearheaded efforts to block solutions, tweeted "thoughts and prayers" for survivors of Hurricane Ida. The American Petroleum Institute, the trade association for oil companies, didn’t even attempt to greenwash: One day after Hurricane Ida made landfall, the group tweeted an appeal for the Biden administration to approve new oil and gas drilling permits.
  • ABORTION RIGHTS UNDER ATTACK: The U.S. Supreme Court allowed a radical anti-abortion law to take effect early Wednesday morning. The move meant a Texas law passed earlier this year now "effectively bans abortion at six weeks, a time at which many people don't realize they're pregnant," HuffPost writes. The law deputizes Texas citizens to sue anyone helping people obtain abortions, with a potential $10,000 reward for those who bring about successful suits.
