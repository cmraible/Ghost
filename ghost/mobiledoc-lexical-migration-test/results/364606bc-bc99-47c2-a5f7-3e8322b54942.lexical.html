Last year was a very good year for Consolidated Edison, the massive investor-owned power company that provides electricity to the New York area. As CEO Timothy Cawley announced during the company’s September earnings call, it just notched its record-setting 47th straight year of dividend increases. That means ConEd is just three boom years away from being crowned a “dividend king,” meaning it will join the elite group of publicly traded companies whose dividends have increased every year for half a century.

But to maintain this profitable winning streak, the power company had to make some very callous business decisions about its customers in the middle of the raging pandemic.

TODAY’S SCOOP:

Last month, Timothy Cawley, CEO of Consolidated Edison, which provides power to the New York area and is one of the largest investor-owned energy companies in the country, had good news to share during the company’s September earnings call. ConEd had increased its shareholder dividends every year for the past 47 years, boasted Cawley, noting it was “the longest such streak of any utility.”

That means ConEd is a “dividend aristocrat,” part of an exclusive club of publicly traded companies whose stocks have produced increasing dividends every year for at least 25 years. The utility is also very close to becoming part of an even more elite group: “dividend kings,” public companies whose dividends have increased every year for 50-plus years.

To maintain that winning streak, ConEd has made some very callous business decisions.

Last year, as the COVID-19 pandemic ravaged New York City, the utility could have chosen to protect the more than two million New Yorkers struggling to pay their utility bills by not increasing its rates. Or it could maintain its attractiveness to long-term investors by hiking people’s utility bills.

Any guess what ConEd chose to do?

That’s right: ConEd customers were hit with a cumulative 13.5 percent electricity hike and 25 percent gas rate hike. At the same time, area residents saw their utility bills’ supply charges — the fee for sending electricity to the customer — nearly double in average cost from February 2020.

As a result, the amount of outstanding utility debt owed by ConEd customers jumped a whopping 189 percent between February 2020 and August 2021. It was the largest debt spike of any utility company in the state — and part of a $20 billion national upswell in utility debt during the pandemic.

Although ConEd and state lawmakers threw customers a bone during the pandemic by suspending service shutoffs for unpaid bills, the measure only allowed customers to defer bill payments, and didn’t cancel the debt altogether. And while ConEd also touted its move to waive late-payment fees during the pandemic, the utility then turned around last spring and petitioned the state to allow it to assess a surcharge on its entire customer base to recover the revenue it lost from this move.

ConEd told state officials it would use the revenues it gained from squeezing customers “to invest in gas and electric infrastructure, as well as fund energy efficient programs.” But those measures also mean massive profits.

A report from New York City Public Advocate Jumaane Williams noted that thanks to the $1.38 billion ConEd earned in net income in 2018, it was able to distribute $846 million to shareholders, shell out manager bonuses, and pay Cawley $9.8 million in CEO compensation.

“When one considers the profits that utilities like ConEd are able to make through these rising rates, the injustice of the mechanisms in place are made even clearer,” noted Williams in the report. “This shareholder and executive compensation amounts to nothing less than a massive wealth transfer from ordinary New Yorkers to the highest of income earners.” The report echoes an increasingly popular clarion call for the public to take back control of investor-owned utilities like ConEd.

In the meantime, Cawley is expecting more boom years to come for ConEd — which likely means more pain for its customers. As the CEO noted in the September earnings call, “Things are picking up and I'm an optimist, but it feels like we're heading in a really good direction.”
