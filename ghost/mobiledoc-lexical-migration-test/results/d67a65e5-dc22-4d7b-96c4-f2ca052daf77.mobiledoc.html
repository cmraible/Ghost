Biden (Somewhat) Flips His Script On Cannabis

On Thursday, President Joe Biden — a leading architect of the war on drugs — announced steps his administration will be taking to address the disastrous effects of mass incarceration and mandatory sentencing laws, which have predominantly targeted Black and Brown communities.

After years of shaping mass incarceration policy in the ’80s and ’90s, Biden is now saying, “Sending people to jail for possessing marijuana has upended too many lives — for conduct that is legal in many states.”

Specifically, Biden plans to pardon all federal offenses of simple marijuana possession, which would help remove barriers for those with records to employment, housing, and educational opportunities. While the sentiment matters, in reality not a singleperson is currently in federal prison for “simple marijuana possession,” meaning Biden’s pardons will actually largely benefit people convicted under District of Columbia drug laws. Furthermore, the announcement specifies that the pardons would not apply to convicted “non-citizens.”

On Thursday evening, Rep. Alexandria Ocasio Cortez (D-N.Y.) tweeted that criminal marijuana records must also be expunged as part of the country’s decriminalization efforts. That’s because expungement would treat marijuana convictions as if they never occurred, and fully remove barriers created by those records.

Biden is also calling on state governors to make pardons and is directing Attorney General Merrick Garland and Secretary of Health and Human Services Xavier Becerra to look into rescheduling cannabis so it is no longer on Schedule I of the Controlled Substances Act, which would mean that marijuana could be more readily researched for medical usage and potentially no longer serve as basis for criminalization under federal law.

Young People Are Embracing Unions

As the uptick in strikes in October — recently dubbed Striketober — makesheadlines and new workplaces launch unionization efforts every week, the Center for American Progress just released a new report on generational “pro-union sentiment.” Gen Z, which includes people 23 years old and younger as of 2020, is more pro-union than nearly every other generation measured.

According to the report, “Members of Gen Z are entering the workforce at a time of growing economic uncertainty, as college degrees no longer promise job stability and young people describe high levels of economic anxiety.”

On average, 64 percent of Gen Z members approve of unions, compared to 61 percent of millennials. These findings build upon 2021 research conducted by Pew Research Center, which found that 69 percent of Americans between ages 18-29 reported feeling that “labor unions have a positive effect on the way things are going in the country,” compared with 55 percent of adults across the board.

California Becomes Latest State To Require Salary Transparency

Last week, California became the latest state to pass a pay disclosure act, joining New York, Washington, Colorado, and several states with similar rules. The new law will require roughly 200,000 companies with at least 15 employees to start disclosing pay ranges on any in-state job postings.

Building on a 2020 mandate targeting wage disparities, companies with more than 100 employees must now publicly report pay data for their employees and contractors by race, ethnicity, and gender. Such salary transparency helps workers avoid getting lowballed in salary negotiations.

“Women, and especially women of color, are literally being robbed of wages every year,” said Jessica Ramey Stender, policy director for Equal Rights Advocates, an advocacy group that co-sponsored the salary bill. “That is money that could go to rent, food, diapers, education, retirement savings. So the time has really come for robust pay equity legislation here in California and beyond.”

Eliminating Food Waste Would Slash Carbon Emissions

A new report finds that zero-waste practices as straightforward as composting could help reduce greenhouse gas emissions released from global waste by as much as 85 percent, roughly the equivalent of eliminating all cars in the U.S.

The Global Alliance for Incinerator Alternatives, an anti-waste initiative, released the report that found that the responsible management of organic waste like food scraps and grass clippings — decomposing matter that releases tons of methane — could have an immense impact on the climate crisis. According to the report, food waste is responsible for nearly 10 percent of climate pollution.

The report examines how municipal efforts to separate food scraps and compost from other waste could reduce urban methane emissions in landfills by 62 percent. Already Seoul, South Korea, seperates 96 percent of its organic waste.

Millions Of Criminal Records To Be Sealed

Millions of Californians will get to seal criminal records from public view after new legislation was signed into law last week. Because of the far reach of the carceral system, nearly eight million Californians, or 1 in 5 state residents, have a record of past arrest or conviction.
