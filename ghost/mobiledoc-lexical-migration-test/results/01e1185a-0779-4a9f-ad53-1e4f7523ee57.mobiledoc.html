Stratechery

Ben Thompson's daily newsletter has a lot to answer for. His — highly successful — brand of in-depth tech analysis, along with Bill Bishop's Sinocism,  paved the way for the current brand of opinion-led newsletters facilitated by platforms like Substack.

Five pieces a week, one of them free. Also available as a podcast for subscribers. This is arguably the “blogpost delivered as newsletter” style.

It is, actually, a good and valuable format, and I'm only grumpy because my in-box is often full of people vying to be the “Stratechery of Media”.

The Quartz Daily Brief

Another landscape-reshaping newsletter. Quartz managed to own the morning for people in the financial sector by doing a comprehensive round-up of what you needed to know before you got to your desk. This is the pioneer of the “morning briefing” style.

It's gone off the boil a bit in recent years — I've included a link to an example of the better original format.

Spectator Lunchtime Update

This is a good example of a newsletter that's designed to fit into the rhythm of people's days. It hits the in-box in the early afternoon (The Spectator's definition of “lunchtime” is a bit broader than mine…), and contains a mix of links, analysis (often an abbreviated version of something on the website), and a cartoon.

It's a gentle nudge to check out the website at a lull in the day, when there's the opportunity to sell the reader on a newsy hook.

Politico London Playbook

This is newsletter-as-product. It's not here to get you to click through to Politico — although you might end up doing so. Instead, it's aiming to become your essential morning briefing on everything you need to know if you're interested in UK politics.

It's information-dense, very much designed for the phone scene, and only leavened with some entertaining writing.

Vox Sentences

This is a newsletter that takes much of its style from its parent publication. Like Vox it aims to explain the news, rather than just reporting it. It does it with a clear structure, and almost obsessive use of bullet points to make information both browsable and digestible.

Telegraph Technology Intelligence

The Telegraph has built out a whole stable of newsletters, under newsletter editor Sarah Ebner (who has now left to run newsletters for the FT).

This is a good example of a niche newsletter from a larger brand, designed to deliver a valuable niche.

Geekout

This is an example of a weekly letter that tries to be completely comprehensive. Matt and Martin try to deliver a newsletter that gives social media managers everything they need to keep abreast with changes in their field. As a result it's bloodyhuge.

For me, it's too much. But then, social media only makes up a small part of what I do. For people whose entire career revolves around it, and who are too busy to keep up with what's happening on every single platform, it's an invaluable resource.

Free, with sponsors.
