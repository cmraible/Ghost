Nobody is owed your attention

A couple of months ago I vaguely disagreed on Facebook with an acquaintance who is fairly big name author these days. He was opining on an area that wasn't his area of expertise — nor mine — but was my wife's. And I passed on her correction. Much to my surprise, despite having happy memories of co-authoring something with him nearly 20 years ago, he unleashed both barrels and me, and a couple of his fans piled in, too.

It was the work of a moment to block him and them. He's clearly not my friend, and he is not owed my attention.

While tracking something else entirely, I discovered that the 'gentleman' in question has a reputation for being completely unable to handle any criticism at all. And yet he spends significant amounts of time assiduously farming attention on social media. I cannot begin to imagine the mental damage that those two behaviours in conjunction must be doing to his psyche.

For years, I've pointed out that journalists need to have a thick skin if they participate on social media. If you work in a profession which is rooted in challenging others, you must be open to some challenge yourself. Indeed, back in the mid-2000s, I spent significant amounts of time persuading colleagues that it was worth engaging with criticisms of their work, because often those criticisms were valid, and we could learn from them.

However, there is clearly a line where valid criticism crosses the line into abuse:

We're living through an age where politicians are raising the temperature of the rhetoric against journalists, and their followers are obediently aping that tone of voice. And there's a fine line to be walked between protecting ourselves and ceding the spaces of public discourse to the polarized factions.

Part of the solution is being more free with the block button. As I said, nobody is owed your attention, and people unable to act in a civilised way do not deserve even an iota of your time. But that approach is, at the very most, a plaster on an open wound of festering toxic discourse.

We clearly need to find a new way forwards - and this looks like a good start.
