Thanks in large part to the relentless pressure of organizations like the Revolving Door Project and Demand Progress, President Joe Biden has appointed several Wall Street hawks to lead top regulatory agencies. Through speeches and statements over the past couple weeks, those appointees are now signaling that they have big plans to protect consumers and tackle corporate crime.

Read about how they plan to use their power and much more in this week’s You Love To See It, exclusive for supporting subscribers below.

Things That You Love To See:

• Securities and Exchange Commission Has Plans To Shed Light On Private Equity — SEC chairman Gary Gensler is signalling that he plans to increase scrutiny on the private equity industry, which is currently seeing a windfallwhile it props up fossil fuels and exploits tax loopholes. Such scrutiny may include disclosure of fees and expenses and increasing transparency of performance metrics, according to a speech Gensler gave to a trade association last week.

Tip Jar

• Consumer Financial Protection Bureau To Tackle Revolving Door — The CFPB, the agency set up by Sen. Elizabeth Warren in the wake of the financial crisis to advocate for consumer interests, published a new ethics guidance this week to tackle the revolving door between the agency and the private sector. “No regulated entity should feel the need to retain the services of a former government employee to meet its legal and regulatory obligations. At the CFPB, agency alumni will not get special treatment. In fact, it will be just the opposite. We will be applying heightened scrutiny to matters and decisions where a party has employed or retained the services of a former employee to ensure that the CFPB is meeting the highest standards of ethics and integrity implemented nearly a decade ago,” the agency said in a statement on the new guidelines.

• Federal Trade Commission Is Taking On Corporate Criminals — The FTC, led by antitrust lawyer Lina Khan, is taking new steps to hold corporations and their executives accountable for their crimes. “At a time when major corporate lawbreakers can treat civil fines as a cost of doing business, government authorities must ensure that criminal conduct is followed by criminal punishment,” Khan said in a statement. “Today the FTC is redoubling its commitment and improving its processes to expeditiously refer criminal behavior to criminal authorities, promoting accountability, and deterrence.”

Organizing Works:

• Build Back Better Passes The House(The Intercept) — The Democrats’ social spending and climate legislation passed the House on Friday, and is now headed to the Senate for a vote. “The bill contains funding for universal pre-K programs, subsidized child care for lower- and middle-class families, an extended child tax credit, the largest federal investment in housing to date, and hundreds of billions of dollars for initiatives to fight climate change. The package also contains a number of narrower proposals meant to shore up long-standing parts of the social safety net, like coverage for hearing needs in Medicare and a program to cover the Medicaid gap, partly paid for by allowing Medicare to negotiate with pharmaceutical companies for lower prices,” The Intercept reports.

• Teamsters United Takes The Wheel (Labor Notes) — The reform coalition within the Teamsters, known as Teamsters for a Democratic Union, has swept the union’s leadership election. “The incoming president is Sean O’Brien, leader of New England Joint Council 10. He says his top priorities are to unite the rank and file to take on employers, organize Amazon and other competitors in the union’s core industries, and withdraw support from politicians who don’t deliver on union demands,” Labor Notes reports.

• John Deere Workers Win Wage Increases, End Strike (Washington Post) — “Workers at Deere & Co., the giant manufacturer of farm and construction equipment, on Wednesday ended a month-long strike that has come to symbolize a new era of labor militancy. Their union, the United Auto Workers, said 10,000 members ratified a new six-year contract offer by a vote of 61 percent to 39 percent. They had rejected two previous offers,” the Washington Post reports. The new contract includes an $8,500 immediate bonus, a 10 percent immediate wage increase, and more.

• Boston Divests City Funds From Fossil Fuels (Common Dreams) — The Boston City Council unanimously passed an ordinance preventing the city treasury from investing funds in the fossil fuel industry. The day before, Green New Deal advocate Michelle Wu was sworn in as Boston’s new mayor, putting the city on the path to becoming a true leader on climate action.
