Social Media's difficult teenage years

Social media has long been the glamorous part of audience engagement work (from the outside, at least), compared to the technical detail of SEO, and the slow, steady work of building newsletters. Indeed, up until a couple of years ago, social media and audience engagement were largely synonymous. Thankfully, that is changing quickly.

Reducing your dependence on social as an acquisition channel feels vital now, as the social media waters feel more choppy than I can ever recall them being in the 20-odd years I've been tracking this stuff.

On the one hand, you have Facebook looking ever-so-slightly desperate in its moves to maintain and protect its position. Instagram seems to be the main "beneficiary" of its efforts, with some fundamental changes, from the advent of TikTok clone Reels, and also a shift in the algorithm, to give you more “discovery” content. A simplistic reading of that change would be that Instagram is, in effect, merging your feed and the discover tab to make room for Reels…

The consequences of Trump's TikTok tantrum

On the other had there's, well, everything about TikTok.

The journey of the last 15 years has been fascinating. When I was running around in 2007 exhorting the likely value of Twitter, it would be fair to say I was met with skepticism. In 2020, we have governments literally fighting over control of social media technology.

For example, China has thrown a massive spanner in the works of the sale of TikTok:

Last week Chinese regulators cast doubt over ByteDance’s planned sale of the US operations of its short-video subsidiary TikTok, by expanding the country’s list of controlled exports to include the algorithms that power the viral video app.

Why the algorithm rather than the data? Simple. It's the algorithm that controls what people see. What people see influences what people think. And China is really active in controlling its image globally.

It's increasingly clear that while the general public perceive social media as a way of keeping up with friends, family and celebs, and publishers see it as a promotional tool. However, governments and other politically active bodies see it as a way to control how people perceive the world.

For too long journalism has let itself been led about by the nose, pandering to the whims of the social media platforms for a taste of that sweet, sweet traffic we all crave. That can't continue.

But walking the fine line of taking advantage of what social media offers us without being consumer and subsumed by it is critical. And really, really hard.

By all means build relationships on social media. But, whatever you do, find ways of moving the most valuable of those readers into a direct relationship, unmediated by social media. Up the passion pathway they go, be it via newsletters, podcasts or any other way you can talk to you readers, without the invisible hand of the algorithm controlling the conversation.
