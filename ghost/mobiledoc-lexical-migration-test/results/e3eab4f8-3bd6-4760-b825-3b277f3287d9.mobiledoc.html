Journalists on social media: a reappraisal

It's clear we're about to go through a reassessment of how journalists should behave on social media — and Twitter in particular. This came up in passing during the discussion about the Reuters Institute Digital News Report yesterday, and is in the news again today:

I've had a couple of interesting discussions about the collapse in trust in journalism over the past 24 hours, and one of the (many) causes several people pinpointed was the blurring of news and opinion both in publications, but especially on social media.

This doesn't have to be the case. There are several journalists I admire — and, in some cases, know personally — whose politics are a mystery to me. They maintain an active and useful social media presence without joining in the open political warfare that exists on Twitter.

And then there are people like Owen Jones or Tom Harwood who are far more polemicists than journalists in the traditional sense. The solution lies far more with the "political blank states" than it does with the political outriders, and it's important we have this conversation right now.

The damage this is doing to the reputation of journalism as a profession needs addressing. And it's making social media an ever-more toxic environment for all journalists.
