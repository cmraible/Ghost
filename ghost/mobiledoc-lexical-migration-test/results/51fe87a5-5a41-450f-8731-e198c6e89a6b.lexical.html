Some rules of thumb

Personal works

Email, at its best, is a very personal medium. Things that make the newsletter sound personal tend to work well. Use of ‚Äúyou‚Äù when you can work it into the subject line can be effective.

A/B Testing

This is an incredibly valuable tool, is you have access to it. Not many of the emergent newsletter platforms do, though, sadly.

In essence, this allows you to segment your newsletter audience, and send one of them a newsletter with, say, a more casual subject line, and the other a more business-like headline. You can then compare open rates between the two, and use that to help refine your sense of the sorts of subject line that work for you audience.

It's the single most useful tool you use to helop refine your understanding of your audience and what works for them, which is why it baffles me that it's available on so many marketing automation platforms, but not editorial newsletter ones.

That said, there are a few caveats worth noting:

  • The tool works better with larger groups rather than small ones, as that reduces oddities thrown up by an uneven spread of the types of subscriber in each group.
  • Some things are easier to A/B test that others (see below).
  • A/B testing can be risky ‚Äî if you're not cautious in your tests, you run the risk of annoying one of the test groups and losing subscribers as a result.

Tests you should be running

  • Subject lines ‚Äî measured through open rates. You can vary the complete subject line, the word order of the subject line, its length, its tone and so on.
  • Intros ‚Äî what style of intro works best for your audience? Look at the options we discussed in the structure section, and consider runing tests. You'll probably measure through aggregate click-through rates or by running long-term tests, with two separate but consistent segments, and see how open rates shift over time.
  • Images ‚Äî Do your readers prefer image-rich emails, or do they like to get down to basics with the text? You'll probably measure through aggregate click-through rates or by running long-term tests, with two separate but consistent segments, and see how open rates shift over time.
  • Timing ‚Äî Measured through open rates and click-through rates. Getting timing right for newsletters can be critical. If you have two theories about the best time to send, then split your audience, and test which time slot gets the better open and click-through rates.

More Advanced Tests

These are tests that you need to run over longer periods of time (weeks to months), and which carry a greater element of risk. Why risk? You are likely to lose some subscribers from the group which has the less successful alternative.

Generally, I advise sticking to set periods for most editorial testing ‚Äî but not for these sorts of newsletter testing. As soon as a clear pattern emerges, end the test, and standardise on the more popular option.

Your list is everything; tests which unduly harm it are a poor trade.

  • Tone and Voice ‚Äî We talked a lot about this in the basic section. If you dobn't have a strong sense of what does work, then test it! Split your group, send newsletters with two different styles of writing, and explore which ones deliver more opens or clickthroughs, ow hich have the lowest churn.
  • Length ‚Äî measured by click-through rates. How long is right for your audience? You have infinite space, but your reader doesn't have infinite time. Do they prefer short and snappy, or extensive and explorable?
  • Newsletter Structure ‚Äî do different elements of your newsletter work better in different places? Can you draw people deeper through it by placing a popular element further down? Split your audience, and test which structure leads to higher open rates or clickthrough rates.

However, sadly, the value of this tool is likely to diminish for some of these areas, especially subject line analysis, in the near future. With the move towards blocking open rate tracking for privacy purposes, its use in subject line testing (which is mainly measured through open rates) become less useful.

You could find a way around that by, for example, keeping the structure of both emails identical, change the subject line, and compare the aggregate click-throughs from the subject lines variants. That should give you some indication of which of the two subject lines work better.
