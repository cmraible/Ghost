Last month, President Joe Biden nominated former Sen. Joe Donnelly (D-Ind.), to serve as  ambassador to the Vatican. As the Indianapolis Starnoted at the time, the nomination of Donnelly made sense because “Faith has been a central theme for both Biden and Donnelly.”

Sure enough, during the 2020 presidential campaign, Donnelly, who is Catholic, wrote an op-ed praising Biden’s faith, noting, “Pope Francis has said, ‘We must restore hope to young people, help the old, be open to the future, and spread love.’ Joe Biden exemplifies that message each day as he works to lead America back to its place as the moral compass for our world and an example for other nations to follow.”

Donnelly’s nomination is notable for another reason: He is a textbook example of one of Washington’s greatest scams, the “shadow lobbying” loophole.

TODAY’S SCOOP:

A new financial disclosure by former Sen. Joe Donnelly, Biden’s nominee for ambassador to the Vatican, sheds light on one of Washington’s greatest scams: the “shadow lobbying” loophole.

Federal ethics rules require former senators like Donnelly to undergo a two-year “cooling off” period before lobbying their old colleagues. To get around that restriction, senators often join big lobbying firms and start advising clients without making any direct contacts that would require them to register as lobbyists.

After losing reelection in 2018, Donnelly quickly joined the lobbying firm Akin Gump Strauss Hauer & Feld LLP. His new Biden administration financial disclosure shows that since then, he’s made $872,000 as a partner at Akin Gump, where he has been advising corporate giants like Amazon, Salesforce, and Anheuser Busch.

Tip Jar

But the most fascinating name in Donnelly’s client list is hedge fund Elliott Management. The firm is best known for buying up countries’ distressed debt for pennies on the dollar and demanding the debt be repaid in full. Elliott made $2.4 billion from its 15-year debt crusade against Argentina — the home country of Pope Francis, who has criticized “savage capitalism.”

Elliott’s leadership also had a hand in Donnelly’s 2018 election loss. The firm’s billionaire founder Paul Singer donated $2 million to the Senate Leadership Fund during the 2018 election cycle, when the super PAC bankrolled $15 million worth of attacks against Donnelly and spent another $2 million to boost his GOP opponent. In his recent disclosure, Donnelly wrote that his work for Elliott has involved “explaining the potential legislative agenda before Congress.”

BONUS SCOOP:

The Partnership for America’s Health Care Future (PAHCF), the anti-Medicare for All front group run by lobbyists for health insurers, drug companies, and hospitals, reported spending nearly $36 million on a “public education” campaign in 2020, according to a new tax return obtained by The Daily Poster.

We already knew PAHCF spent millions on TV ads attacking Medicare for All during the Democratic presidential primary campaign, but it appears the propaganda campaign went much further. Their tax return says they spent that money on grants to other nonprofits and “digital and print advertising, social media, and direct mail to educate the American public.”
