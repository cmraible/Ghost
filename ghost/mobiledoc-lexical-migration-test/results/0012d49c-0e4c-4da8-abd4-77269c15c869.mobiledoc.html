TODAY’S SCOOP:

During the first half of this year, the Democratic Congressional Campaign Committee (DCCC), the party committee that works to elect Democratic candidates for House seats, brought in $483,000 worth of checks raised by a handful of business lobbyists with close ties to fossil fuel companies, pharmaceutical giants, and private equity firms, federal election records show.

Because party committees are subject to contribution limits, lobbyists sometimes work to collect checks from their colleagues and personal networks so they can pool larger cash totals. Bundling checks in this way for committees like the DCCC that serve as essential party infrastructure is a key method that corporate lobbyists use to buy access with lawmakers.

The new filings reveal that lobbyists Zachary Pfister and David Reid, from the legal juggernaut Brownstein Hyatt Farber Schreck, used this process to raise a combined $283,000 for the DCCC during the first half of this year.

Pfister has lobbied for the American Petroleum Institute, a major lobbying group for oil and gas companies, and pharmaceutical companies Johnson & Johnson, Eli Lilly, Novartis, and AbbVie. He’s also lobbied for Tyson Foods, gig economy company Uber, hospital chain Tenet Healthcare, and private equity firms Ares Capital and Leonard Green & Partners.

Reid and Pfister have both lobbied for McDonald’s, Starbucks, and casino companies MGM and Caesars. Reid has also lobbied for health insurer Centene, Royal Caribbean Cruises, and the telecom lobbying group NCTA.

Along with Reid and Pfister’s bundling efforts for the DCCC, Heather Podesta, the founder and CEO of Invariant, has raised roughly $114,000 for the committee this year. She has lobbied for the controversial surveillance software company Palantir, billionaire Elon Musk’s space company SpaceX, the National Association of Realtors, and the Business Roundtable, a lobbying group for corporate CEOs.

Steve Elmendorf, a partner at Subject Matter, has raised another $63,000 for the DCCC this year. Elmendorf has lobbied for Amazon, Facebook, BP, Pfizer, and UnitedHealthcare. He has represented Wall Street powerhouses like Goldman Sachs and the Blackstone Group. Elmendorf has also worked for lobbying groups like the American Investment Council, which represents private equity firms, and the Federation of American Hospitals, which represents investor-owned hospital companies.

John Michael Gonzalez, a partner at Tiber Creek Group, raised $23,500 for the DCCC. His clients have included the Business Roundtable, a lobbying group for corporate CEOs; Pharmaceutical Research and Manufacturers of America (PhRMA), which lobbies for drugmakers; and the Managed Funds Association, a lobbying group for hedge funds.

TODAY’S FLASHBACK:

Last month on Meet the Press, Senator Rob Portman of Ohio, a leading Republican in the bipartisan negotiations over President Joe Biden’s proposed infrastructure package, said strengthening the IRS’ enforcement arm was an important part of the proposal.

Doing so could raise up to $100 billion over the next ten years without increasing taxes, since it would help close the “tax gap” between what taxpayers owe and what they pay.

Much of that gap is due to the wealthy, who on average pay less in taxes than typical American workers. A recent bombshell ProPublica report found that billionaires like Jeff Bezos, Elon Musk, and Warren Buffett in some years paid next to nothing to the IRS.

“They should be paying taxes,” said Portman when asked if Jeff Bezos and Amazon should be contributing more to the country’s infrastructure. “And that’s actually part of our proposal, too. We have about a $63 billion pay-for that is hoping to close the tax gap that assumes about a $40 billion investment in the IRS, including, by the way, in better taxpayer service, which is really important right now, but also enforcement, and let`s be sure that we`re closing that tax gap.”

Now, however, closing that tax enforcement gap appears to be a dealbreaker for Portman and his colleagues.

Over the weekend, Republicans forced the bipartisan group to remove increasing IRS funding from the proposal because, as Portman told CNN, the idea faced “pushback.” Earlier this month, major anti-tax conservative groups sent a letter to GOP leadership calling the infrastructure package a “basic betrayal of conservative values” and demanding “no additional funding for the Internal Revenue Service.”

Lawmakers are still scrambling to find alternative ways to fund the infrastructure package. Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer (D-N.Y.) is set to open debate on the infrastructure legislation today.

  • JUSTICE DEPT. SLAMS PURDUE OPIOID BANKRUPTCY PLAN: In new court briefs filed in New York, representatives of the U.S. Justice Department blasted a proposed bankruptcy settlement for Purdue Pharma, the makers of OxyContin, for potentially granting sweeping immunity to the Sacklers, the billionaire family behind the pharmaceutical giant, despite their prominent role in the country’s opioid crisis. U.S. Trustee William Harrington, who oversees federal bankruptcy cases in the region, called the potential settlement, which would protect the Sacklers and contacts in their business empire from lawsuits in exchange for the family contributing less than half of the $10 billion they allegedly made from opioid sales, “nothing less than an illegal, court-ordered discharge of a potentially limitless group of non-debtors.” The U.S. Attorney for the Southern District of New York concurred, noting in a filing that the plan would violate the constitutional rights of those impacted by the opioid crisis. According to NPR, bankruptcy Judge Robert Drain “is widely expected to approve the Purdue Pharma settlement” at an August 9 hearing.
