Emerging Independent Media

I've used a personal classification system to judge media for years now. Any media entity is usually in one of three states as we move through the digitalisation of everything:

  • Emergence
  • Evolution
  • Extinction

I'm planning to write more about how the pandemic is accelerating all three of those, but let's start off with Emergence.

Emerging media is going through a quiet boom right now. Lots of laid-off journalists or under-employed freelancers are experimenting with their own newsletters or podcasts. Newsletter platform Substack has just announced its next fellowship for independent writers, for example:

It's vaguely reminiscent of the original blog boom in the early 2000s, which was born of the dotcom crash leaving loads of techies with times on their hands. They created both blogs and the tools to create them with, and were the foundation layer of what later became known as Web 2.0.

One of the interesting things about the pandemic destroying two of the three legs sustaining most media businesses - events and advertising - is the invountary freeing-up of talent to create their own thing in the pandemic recession. Most of these ventures will stumble quickly, and others will vanish as soon as the creator is re-employed. But some will stick, and we'll have a new wave of emerging media to get excited about.

Why am I writing about this today?

Because of this:

We've seen a huge consolidation of talent in podcasts - but the big companies still only represent about 50% of the market. And, without doubt, some of the most interesting experimentation is happening in the 49% of indie podcasts. My own listening is well over 70% indies.

As long as podcasting stays an open format, with easy-to-use apps, we can expect it to be a reasonably easy place for people to start creating their own media products.

Google opens its wallet

Well, now. This is just what publishers in Europe have been demanding for some time. Or, at least it is on the surface.

When you dig deeper, it's a bit more complicated:

[…] the search giant is creating a licensing program to pay publishers "for high-quality content" as a part of a new news product launching later this year.

Emphasis mine. It looks like Google is launching something more akin to Apple News (yes, Google has had similar products before. No, that's never stopped it trying multiple versions. Look how many messaging apps there have been over the years…)

So, as Joshua Benton points out, this has more than the whiff of a PR move designed to head off regulation:

Benton:

From the duopoly’s perspective, the biggest problem with paying for all the news coursing through their digital veins isn’t the money. (They have plenty of money.) It’s that paying for news in any systemic way would attack their core advantage as platforms: organizing other people’s content.

For Google, in particular, it's really important that they stay as a major intermediary between us and the audience. For us, it's important to make sure we have as many direct relationships as possible. Google's money shouldn't seduce us away from that goal.
