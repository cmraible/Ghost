The pandemic shows the need to embrace explainer journalism

Once you start paying attention to SEO, you realise how important it is to update your content online. And then, if you're smart, a little light goes off in your head, and you realise how very different digital content is from its printed equivalent. One of the great boons of the digital format is the ability to update things without having to entirely rewrite them - to see your website as a living document, not a moment in time caught in paper aspic.

Now, let's turn that idea around the other way: what's the price of not updating things?

In a pandemic, the answer is, of course, intentionally or not, you end up communicating poor, outdated information.

And then, as these tweets highlight, you have a problem:

Six years ago, I wrote a piece on explainer journalism for journalism.co.uk. We have not moved on those ideas nearly quickly enough as an industry yet. We still remain deeply attached to the idea of a rolling series of updated news stories, rather than single, updated resources. The two are not mutually exclusive, of course. The single explainer can link to the news story, and the news story back to the explainer. But are you leaving stories up on your site with outdated Covid-19 info in them? If so, are you modifying them in some way to allow people to find the more updated information?

If not, you are unintentionally contributing to misinformation. People just don't pay attention to dates when they share things. They don't. This is one of those changes in thinking the pandemic should be accelerating - but it isn't.
