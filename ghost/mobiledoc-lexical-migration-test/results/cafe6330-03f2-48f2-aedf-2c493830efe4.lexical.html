The arc of the moral universe is long, but it bends towards justice. The one percent wants us as discombobulated as possible so that we don’t have the energy to push forward, even with so much at stake. But despite their efforts to wear us down, good things are happening!

Congress finally took action to save the U.S. Postal Service, the number of unionized Starbucks has doubled, teachers in Minneapolis are striking for their students, Santa Barbara County stopped Exxon in its quest to reopen a dangerous pipeline, and the D.C. city council passed one of the strongest renter protections since the pandemic began.

All that and much more in today’s You Love To See It, exclusive for supporting subscribers below.

Workers Winning

Congress finally repealed the nonsensical requirement that the Postal Service pre-fund its retiree health benefits up to 75 years in the future. The legislation also protected Saturday deliveries. This will go a long way to ensuring the long-term survival of America’s oldest social program.

Tip Jar

The number of unionized Starbucks locations in the country has now doubled. More than 100 stores have launched unionization campaigns, and Starbucks workers in Denver have gone on strike against Starbucks’ union-busting efforts. Local shift supervisor Michaela Sellaro toldFOX31: “We are striking today to call attention to Starbucks’ retaliatory and intimidating actions, including but not limited to: cutting hours … baseless final warnings for (alleged violations of) vague and inconsistently enforced policies, and spying on and otherwise monitoring pro-union partners and their activities.”

In other union news, 4,500 teachers in Minneapolis are on strike to demand “reduced caseloads for special education teachers, school psychologists, social workers and counselors. [The union is] also seeking increased reserves of special education aides and others to help teachers,” according to the Associated Press.

Colorado’s Attorney General Stands Up On Student Debt

While Biden continues to violate his oft-repeated pledge to cancel $10,000 of student loan debt, Colorado Attorney General Phil Weiser is putting pressure on Colorado’s U.S. Attorney to stop hurting student loan borrowers in bankruptcy.

“The past two years highlighted the challenges student loan borrowers can face and the importance of grace for those in difficult circumstances,” Weiser said in a press release. “It is important that student loan borrowers facing bankruptcy have a clear path to having their loans discharged as promised in the Bankruptcy Code. I encourage the Justice Department to carefully consider its role in such cases and to extend grace to student loan borrowers who face an ‘undue hardship’ in repaying their loans.”

Environmental Advocates Score Victories Over Big Oil

While the war in Ukraine has emboldened oil and gas companies and their allies to call for reduced regulation, the Santa Barbara County Board of Supervisors isn’t having it. They just rejected an ExxonMobil trucking proposal that was integral to reopening a pipeline that had been shut down in 2015 due to a massive oil spill.

In other good news for the environment, the Colorado Oil and Gas Commission rejected a proposal from Anadarko subsidiary Kerr-McGree to drill 33 wells in a residential area in Firestone, Colorado, 30 miles north of Denver.

Also, major institutional investors are pressuring the federal Securities and Exchange Commission — the agency that regulates financial markets — to take further action on climate disclosures, and health care workers are taking on fossil fuel-addicted hospital investment executives.

Populists Thinking Strategically

As higher gas prices continue to squeeze working class Americans, some lawmakers are fighting back. Reflecting some populist sophistication, Sens. Sherrod Brown (D-Ohio) and Sheldon Whitehouse (D-R.I.) introduced legislation to subject oil companies to an excess profits tax that will be immediately rebated to consumers.

While the housing crisis continues onward, some local officials understand the gravity of the crisis and are taking action. On March 4, ​​“the D.C. Council permanently barred landlords from filing evictions against tenants who owe less than $600, sealed some eviction records, and further protected voucher holders during the rental screening process in an omnibus tenant protection bill… the bill is one of the strongest and most comprehensive bills in the country related to tenant protections in some time,” according to DCist.
