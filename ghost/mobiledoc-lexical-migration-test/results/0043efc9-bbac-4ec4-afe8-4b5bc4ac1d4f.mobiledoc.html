Local journalists under sustained online attack

New research highlights the depressing amount of abuse local journalists face:

The most common platform for abuse is Facebook, with 89% of respondents receiving abuse on this platform. For Twitter, it’s 67%. Eighty per cent say they receive abuse in the comments sections of their newspaper websites.

Three thoughts:

  1. The fact that Facebook is the biggest problem should dispell the old myth that doing away with anonymity woule resolve trolling problems
  2. As ever, don't have a comment section if you're not prepared to manage it. (Drive-by politcial abuse is one of the reasons I've restriced commenting here to signed-in subscribers. Try it! ⬇️)
  3. I remain concerned that, as local journalists become ever more distant from the communities they serve, this is only going to get worse.

Taking Discourse off SubStack

A counterpoint to yesterday's piece, and in particular my worries about Substack becoming a single point of failure for independent journalism: Discourse is leaving SubStack and relaunching on a WordPress-based stack, facilitated by Lede.

It's worth watching how this goes, but it's worth noting that SubStack is still very much a newsletter platform, with a web presence as an afterthough. It's not — yet — a good home for more sophisticated offerings.
