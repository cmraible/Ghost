Plymouth made the same mistake as sister division Dodge by gambling on much smaller 1962 cars with different styling. Because of this, Dodge remained ninth in industry production, but Plymouth had plunged from fourth to eighth.

Fortunately, Chrysler had a new styling chief, Elwood Engel, just recruited from Ford, and his hurry-up efforts produced nice, face-lifted 1963 cars that helped Plymouth reclaim fourth again. A conservative restyle graced the Plymouth Fury for 1963, with four headlights and vertical front fender lines.

As in 1962, the top of the line at Plymouth was the Sport Fury, which returned from 1962 as a convertible and hardtop coupe with standard all-vinyl bucket-seat interior and huge performance potential. Unlike regular Furys, the Sport Furys had a standard V8 in the form of a mild 230-horsepower 318, a 265-bhp 361,a 383 ci 330 hp “Golden Commando,” and on to the newly enlarged 426 big-blocks packing up to 425 hp with the "ram induction."
