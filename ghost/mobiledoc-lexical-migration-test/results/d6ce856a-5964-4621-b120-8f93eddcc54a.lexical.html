Good things are happening! American Rescue Plan Act funds are being put to work for homes and teachers. In Pennsylvania, residents are getting much needed funding for home improvements. Across the country, public schools are offering big signing bonuses to hire much needed teachers amid severe shortages. Also, Uber and Lyft drivers in California have organized and secured workplace and health protections for the first time ever. Meanwhile, Chipotle workers just won a major settlement.

Pennsylvanians Get Home Repair Funds

A new Whole Home Repairprogram will use $125 million in American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) funds to help Pennsylvanians fix their homes. Philadelphia in particular has some of the oldest houses in the country, and an estimated 280,000 occupied units in Pennsylvania have moderate to severe physical deficiencies.

In an effort to prevent low and moderate-income homeowners from having to relocate, they will be able to use up to $50,000 to repair anything from leaking pipes to cracked ceilings. Funds will go toward training workers and helping homeowners afford weatherization improvements to make homes more energy-efficient.

The program also aims to tackle gentrification by helping homeowners catch up on costly repairs so that they don’t feel pressure to sell to developers targeting their homes. The program is slated to be especially helpful for older Pennsylvanians who may own homes but lack the income to maintain them.

Though the program is a one-time round of funding, organizers and representatives are working on making it part of a long-term strategy to address housing. “We know we have a multi-billion dollar problem on our hand, and we have $125 million of one-time funding. So, we really have to treat this as a pilot program,” said Democratic state Rep. Sara Innamorato.

Signing Bonuses For Teachers

As public schools across the country face a severe teacher shortage, ARPA funds are being used to provide competitive signing bonuses to new hires. Taos, New Mexico, is promising a $50,000 starting salary for every new teacher hired, with an additional $10,000 signing bonus. Similarly, a $10,000 signing bonus is being offered by Stanly County Schools in North Carolina.

Florida Enacts Its Strongest Climate Policy In Over A Decade

On Tuesday, Florida’s new law to transition the state to 100 percent renewable energy by 2050 went into effect. Youth activists fought hard to make this climate action a reality. About 200 young Floridians worked with the public interest law firm Our Children’s Trust to petition for new rulemaking, which pushed the Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services to propose these goals in April.

Workplace Protections Extended To Gig Workers

California’s workplace safety regulators cited Uber and Lyft for safety violations, including their failure to provide masks and gloves to prevent the spread of COVID-19 and for excluding drivers from general injury and illness prevention plans.

Rideshare Drivers United, a group of Lyft and Uber drivers who have organized to demand full labor rights, launched a campaign for health protections during a COVID surge in February. After their petition was rejected, they filed the complaints with state regulators, which resulted in this victory.

“This is the first time that a state safety agency has extended workplace protections to gig workers, and challenges the companies’ (claim) that they have no responsibility to ensure safety of drivers,” Rideshare Drivers United said in a statement. “Lyft and Uber are again breaking the law by not notifying drivers about these citations, as required under California law.”

Chipotle Workers Win Settlement Money

13,000 Chipotle workers will receive $20 million in settlement money after a four-year investigation by New York City's Department of Consumer and Worker Protection (DCWP) found Chipotle to be in violation of workplace protection laws around fair scheduling and paid sick leave.

“This almost feels unreal. When you’re in the thick of it, like going to work each day and going through the motions, you think no one is paying attention,” Chipotle worker Yeral Martinez said in a statement. “But this settlement proves that we’re not invisible. Companies can’t treat their workers as disposable. We are worthy of respect, dignity, and fair schedules!”

The DCWP had calculated that 6,500 employees were owed about $150 million — “approximately $23,061.00 relief owed per affected employee.” While this week’s settlement falls far short of that estimate, Chipotle workers are continuing to organize for more, working with local 32BJ-Service Employees International Union as they seek greater protections. Just last month, a Chipotle location near Lansing, Michigan, petitioned for a union.
