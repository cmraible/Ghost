TODAY’S STAT:

It is going to cost the United States $634 billion over the next decade to maintain its nuclear forces, according to a new Congressional Budget Office (CBO) report. Almost two-thirds of those costs are for the Department of Defense, mostly to maintain ballistic missile submarines and intercontinental ballistic missiles. About one-third is for the Department of Energy.

That’s 1.5 times the cost of all of the $1,400 stimulus checks that were sent to people through the American Rescue Plan earlier this year.

It’s nearly 14 times the $47 billion that Congress has spent so far this year helping Americans who are behind on rent.

It’s over one-third of the cost of cancelling the $1.7 trillion in student debt held by Americans, most of which is never going to be repaid.

The new CBO estimate represents a 28-percent increase over the last 10-year estimate that the CBO made on U.S. nuclear forces two years ago. And it comes just weeks after a new analysis from the Center for International Policy, a foreign policy think tank in Washington, found that “In 2020 alone, the CEOs of the [Pentagon’s] top five contractors received a total of $105.4 million in compensation.” About half of the Pentagon’s budget goes directly to corporations such as Lockheed Martin and General Dynamics, according to the report.

So far, the deficit scolds who wanted to narrow eligibility for stimulus checks, student debt cancellation, or rent relief haven’t complained about the spending.

TODAY’S SCOOP:

President Joe Biden’s former longtime legislative director has been directly lobbying the executive office on behalf of the nursing home industry, according to federal records reviewed by The Daily Poster. The lobbying has occurred as the Biden administration has declined to rescind a Trump administration guidance that is helping nursing home executives shield themselves from legal consequences for COVID-19 deaths.

At issue is a 2005 law, the Public Readiness and Emergency Preparedness Act, known as the PREP Act, which authorizes federal authorities to grant certain types of legal immunity during public health emergencies. This past January, President Donald Trump’s Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) issued an advisory opinion under the PREP Act granting immunity to nursing homes for some of their business decisions during the pandemic.

“That HHS opinion has since become a core part of the legal strategy for many of the nursing homes as they argue for immunity,” Politico reported last week, adding, “And while many federal judges have so far rejected nursing homes’ arguments, trial lawyers, advocates and other experts say the strategy is extending litigation through appeals.”

The Biden administration has been touting its use of the PREP Act as part of three measures designed to authorize more professionals to serve as vaccinators. However, the administration has so far declined to use its power to revoke or change the Trump-era HHS guidance thatinterpreted the PREP Act differently from the courts, which have ruled against nursing homes invoking the law to claim immunity.

At the same time, the nursing home industry’s chief lobbying group, the American Health Care Association, has ramped up its lobbying in recent months, including lobbying on liability issues. In December, the group hired Sudafi Henry, who previously served as Biden’s director of legislative affairs when Biden was vice president.

Henry lobbied Congress on behalf of the association in the last month of 2020. Then, once Biden took office, he began directly lobbying the executive office of the president on “issues involving COVID-19 relief,” according to lobbying disclosures.

Henry’s lobbying firm, theGROUP, did not respond to The Daily Poster’s request for comment.
