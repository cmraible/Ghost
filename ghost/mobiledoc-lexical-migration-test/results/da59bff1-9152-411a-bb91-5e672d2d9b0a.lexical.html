Victory For The Corinthian 15

The Education Department announced it will clear the debts of 560,000 borrowers who had attended Corinthian Colleges, a for-profit higher ed chain that collapsed in 2015 in the wake of investigations into its illegal recruitment tactics and misleading pledges to students about course programming and graduates’ career prospects. The $5.8 billion in Corinthian student debt will be automatically canceled, so those who enrolled in the chain need not apply to have their debts wiped out.

The push for cancellation was ignited when Corinthian 15, a group of students supported by the debtors’ union Debt Collective, initiated a debt strike 7 years ago and refused to pay their federal loans.

Key to the students’ fight was a provision they unearthed in the law governing their loans, which allows borrowers to request loan forgiveness from the government if they were severely misled by their school.

The major move comes as President Biden faces mounting pressure to cancel student loan debt, a policy popular with many Americans.

Germany’s Promising Public Transit Plan

Germans will enjoy reduced train fares this summer, thanks to a new law passed by the country’s parliament. The initiative, effective as of June 1, is a way of increasing public transit to protect the environment and reduce dependency on Russian oil. Fares will be cut by more than 90 percent in some cities and 50 trains will be added to accommodate new riders.

Vermont’s Bipartisan Child Tax Credit

As part of a broader tax cut package, Republican Vermont Gov. Phil Scott just signed into law a child tax credit that will send $1,000 for every child 5 years of age and under in Vermont households making up to $125,000. “The final version of H.510 will spend $40 million, the bulk of which — $32 million — will pay for the child tax credit, which is estimated would benefit over 30,000 children,” according to VTDigger.

Vermont is the latest state to introduce a fully refundable child tax credit since Congress failed in December to continue the enhanced federal CTC, which lifted millions of children out of poverty.

Progress In New York

While New York has had a questionable legislativetrack record lately, a couple surprisingly good things have also occurred under the dome.

For starters, the state legislature just passed a bill to create more transparency around company ownership — which, as the American Financial Reform coalition put it, “will make it harder for private equity to hide in the shadows!”

Plus, a bill to update New York’s archaic “wrongful death” statute cleared the state Senate and Assembly. If it’s signed into the law, the bill will extend the state’s statute of limitations to claim wrongful deaths to 3.5 years — something powerful health care and nursing home interests have been fighting for years, to avoid liability for negligence. Passage of the legislation will be, as Assemblyman Ron Kim noted, “a game changer,” especially in light of the thousands of preventable deaths that stemmed from New York nursing homes putting profits over the health and safety of residents during the COVID-19 pandemic.

In Other News…

• Academy Museum of Motion Pictures workers have formed a union. “In an industry that saw closures and mass layoffs at the beginning of the COVID pandemic, cultural workers have found power through forming a union, using their unified voice to set industry standards that improve job protections, safety, wages and benefits,” said the American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees (AFSCME) in a statement on Wednesday. Also on the labor organizing front, 900 Rochester General nurses in New York are forming the independent Rochester Union of Nurses and Allied Professionals.

• Nearly 50 Democrats and Republicans have signed on as co-sponsors of newly introduced federal legislation that invokes constitutional war powers to end U.S. military involvement in Saudi Arabia’s devastating war in Yemen. As The Intercept’s Sara Sirota tweeted, “The war, especially Riyadh’s airstrikes and port blockades, have killed hundreds of thousands of civilians, mostly children dying from starvation and disease.” The legislation opens a window for President Joe Biden to finally fulfill his campaign pledge to end U.S. involvement in the war.
